\begin{abstract}
\noindent \textbf{Context:} Topological data analysis (TDA) offers geometric approaches to analyzing market correlation structure through persistent homology. This study investigates conditions under which TDA can reliably detect market regime shifts---not to generate trading alpha, but to characterize \textit{when} correlation networks become unstable.

\textbf{The Problem:} Initial application of persistent homology to mixed-sector equity baskets produced unstable topological features (CV $> 0.65$), rendering regime detection unreliable. The instability stems from correlation heterogeneity: combining assets with different baseline correlations (e.g., Technology $\rho \approx 0.75$ with Consumer Goods $\rho \approx 0.35$) creates noise that overwhelms structural signal.

\textbf{The Discovery:} Through systematic investigation, we identify a \textbf{correlation homogeneity requirement}: topological features become stable and interpretable only when computed on asset groups with mean pairwise correlation $\rho > 0.50$. Below this threshold, features reflect estimation noise rather than market structure.

\textbf{The Result:} Computing topology \textit{within} homogeneous sectors (rather than cross-sector) yields stable features (CV $< 0.45$) that track regime shifts. Machine learning confirms topology captures regime structure ($F_1 = 0.578$) though directional prediction remains near-random (AUC $\approx 0.52$), consistent with efficient market limits on price forecasting. A theoretical bound ($\text{CV}(H_1) \leq \alpha/\sqrt{\rho(1-\rho)}$) motivated by spectral graph theory provides a framework for understanding why correlation homogeneity is necessary for topological stability.

\textbf{Contribution:} This work identifies \textbf{boundary conditions} for applying TDA to financial data: (1) correlation homogeneity as a necessary condition, (2) a practical threshold ($\rho_c \approx 0.50$) for deployment, and (3) theoretical foundations explaining the correlation-stability relationship. The framework is suited for \textit{regime detection and risk monitoring}, not directional trading.

\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent\textbf{Scope:} This is exploratory research establishing necessary conditions for TDA applicability, not a validated trading system.
\end{abstract}

\subsection*{Why Sector-Specific Works}

Sector-specific universes exhibit three critical properties:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Higher baseline correlation} ($\rho > 0.6$ vs $0.4$ cross-sector) from shared fundamental drivers
    \item \textbf{Coherent eigenstructure} (eigenvalue concentration vs dispersion)
    \item \textbf{Stable topological features} (CV $< 0.45$ vs $> 0.65$)
\end{enumerate}

This correlation-stability mechanism:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Shows consistency in simulated cross-market scenarios ($\rho \approx -0.97$ correlation-CV across 11 scenarios)
    \item Is supported by machine learning ($F_1$ improves $40\times$, though AUC $\approx 0.52$ indicates weak directional predictability)
    \item Is motivated by mathematical theory (random matrix theory, spectral graph analysis)
\end{itemize}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Motivation}

Traditional quantitative trading strategies rely on correlation matrices to measure market risk and construct diversified portfolios. However, correlation-based approaches face a fundamental limitation: they capture only \textbf{pairwise relationships}, missing the higher-order structure that emerges during market stress.

During the 2008 financial crisis, seemingly diversified portfolios collapsed as correlations that appeared stable suddenly spiked to near-unity. Credit default swaps, mortgage-backed securities, and equity markets---assets considered uncorrelated---moved in lock-step, creating catastrophic losses for institutional investors who believed their correlation-based risk models protected them.

\textbf{The core problem:} Correlations measure linear dependence between two assets, but they cannot detect system-wide contagion until it has already occurred. By the time correlation matrices show stress ($\rho > 0.9$), it is too late to reposition.

\textbf{Topological Data Analysis (TDA)} offers an alternative: instead of measuring pairwise relationships, TDA examines the \textbf{shape of the correlation network}â€”detecting loops, voids, and connected components that signal when markets transition from calm to stressed regimes. Persistent homology, the core mathematical tool of TDA, can identify structural instability \textit{before} correlations spike, providing a potential early-warning system for regime shifts.

\subsection{Research Question}

This thesis addresses one central question:

\begin{quote}
\textit{Can topological data analysis generate tradeable signals through detecting regime shifts in equity market correlation structure?}
\end{quote}

This deceptively simple question requires answering several sub-questions:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item \textbf{Does topology contain tradeable information?} (Section~\ref{sec:sector}) Or is it merely a noisy re-parameterization of correlations?

    \item \textbf{What drives topology stability?} (Sections~\ref{sec:sector}--\ref{sec:crossmarket}) Why do some markets produce stable topological features while others do not?

    \item \textbf{Can machine learning extract topology signals efficiently?} (Section~\ref{sec:ml}) Is topology fundamentally limited, or poorly exploited?

    \item \textbf{Why does the correlation-stability relationship exist?} (Section~\ref{sec:theory}) Is this an empirical accident or a mathematical necessity?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Core Contribution}

\textbf{The central contribution of this thesis is identifying correlation homogeneity as a necessary condition for topological stability, with a practical threshold around $\rho \approx 0.50$.} Below this threshold, topological features become unstable and untradeable; above it, persistent homology provides reliable regime detection signals.

\subsection{Reader's Roadmap}

This thesis proceeds in two phases: an \textbf{initial failed strategy} (Sections 2--5) and a \textbf{systematic investigation} that transforms the failure into a general framework (Sections 6--12).

    \item Correlation threshold ($\tau$): 0.3

\textbf{Key Patterns:}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{$\rho$-CV inverse relationship}: Simulated scenarios exhibit strong negative correlation between mean $\rho$ and topology CV (Pearson $\rho \approx -0.97$), consistent with U.S. sector empirical pattern ($\rho = -0.95$, Figure 7.2)

\item \textbf{Critical threshold pattern}: The 10 scenarios with $\rho \geq 0.50$ show CV $< 0.55$ (stable topology regime), while the 1 scenario with $\rho < 0.50$ shows CV = 0.62 (unstable regime)

\item \textbf{Cross-asset consistency}: The correlation-stability relationship holds across simulated equity sectors, international indices, and cryptocurrency baskets, suggesting the pattern may generalize beyond U.S. markets if correlation structure is similar

\item \textbf{Threshold transition}: Crypto basket ($\rho = 0.48$, just below threshold) shows CV = 0.62, suggesting $\rho_c \approx 0.50$ acts as gradual transition point rather than sharp cutoff
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Interpretation Caveat:} These simulated scenarios demonstrate that \textit{if} international/crypto markets exhibit correlation structures similar to calibration assumptions, \textit{then} the topology stability patterns should generalize. Actual market validation would require live data collection and backtesting, which is beyond this thesis scope.
